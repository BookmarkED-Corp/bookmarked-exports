Bookmarked Vision Cascade

The canonical vision document for the Bookmarked Operating System. What we're building, why it matters, and how it cascades from the ultimate vision to today's product.

March 18, 2026  ·  Steve Wandler, Founder  ·  Status: Active
Compliance opened the door. Discovery is what's on the other side.

We believe that protecting a book's content and connecting it to the right reader are not opposing goals — they're the same goal, approached with intelligence.

We believe that the people who steward books — librarians, teachers, parents — shouldn't have to choose between compliance and discovery. They should have tools that make both possible, at the same time, without compromise.

For the first time in history, we have the technology to understand what's inside a book, understand who's reading it, and match the two — with intelligence, with respect for copyright, and within the rules that govern access.

What's missing isn't the technology. It's the platform — the intelligence layer purpose-built for the people who steward human knowledge.

We're building the intelligence layer between every book and every reader.

The Problem

The Three-Sided Problem Nobody Else Can Solve

Bookmarked solves a problem that no one else can solve because they only see one side of it.

1

Protect the Book

The content is the author's and publisher's intellectual property. Copyright is sacred. We never compromise, expose, or diminish it.

But books also need protection from wrongful censorship — removal driven by opinion, bias, or incomplete data rather than legitimate regulatory grounds. Today, PEN America tracks bans and challenges but not resolutions. A book removed for review and returned to the shelf still appears "banned." Districts see the list and refuse to order anything on it — not because the book is inappropriate, but because they don't want the scrutiny.

The result: de facto censorship driven by incomplete data. There is no case law, no single source of truth for what actually happened to a challenged book. Did the district review it and keep it? Move it to a more appropriate grade level? Remove it permanently? Nobody knows. Bookmarked becomes that source of truth — tracking not just challenges, but outcomes.

2

Protect the Reader

Readers deserve to know what they're getting into before they read — the way moviegoers know a film's rating before buying a ticket. But books have no equivalent system, and creating one risks being seen as censorship.

The solution isn't a gatekeeper deciding what's "appropriate." It's personalized, contextual transparency: helping every reader make an informed choice without a human or algorithm deciding for them. We don't want to be the TikTok algorithm for books — pushing content based on engagement. We want to help readers find what they'll love, within the frameworks that apply to them.

For students in public schools, this takes on legal dimensions. Certain content is not legally permitted in certain school settings — and the rules vary by state, by grade level, and by context. A 5th grader and a 12th grader in the same district may have different legal access to the same book. The platform must understand both the content and the context.

3

Match Within Regulatory Frameworks

This is what makes the problem hard and why others haven't solved it. The regulatory landscape is complex and expanding: 26 states have book compliance legislation today, projected to reach 35-40 by 2029. Each state has different requirements. Some create personal accountability for educators. All create operational burden on districts that are already stretched thin.

Bookmarked must navigate all of this — protecting copyright, enabling informed choice, respecting freedom of speech, preventing both illegal censorship and illegal access — while being seen as an agnostic platform, not a gatekeeper. We don't restrict books. We don't approve books. We provide intelligence that enables informed human decisions, within the regulatory frameworks that apply.

The Vision

What We're Building

Bookmarked is the intelligence layer that protects books, protects readers, and connects them — within the regulatory, copyright, and ethical frameworks that make this problem uniquely complex.

We are the agnostic platform. Publishers recommend their own books (bias toward their catalog). Distributors recommend what they distribute (bias toward their inventory). Librarians curate based on their training and personal reading (structural bias — 10,000 books chosen from 40+ million, based on what data?). Bookmarked recommends based on the actual students, the actual collection, and the actual regulatory requirements. No commercial bias. No personal bias. Data-backed decisions.

We don't replace librarians, teachers, or curators. We give them the data infrastructure they never had — so their professional judgment is informed by actual student composition, actual collection gaps, actual regulatory requirements, and actual usage patterns. The AI does the work humans literally cannot do. The humans do the work that only humans should do.

The Spotify Analogy — With Regulatory Complexity

Spotify didn't kill the music industry. It made discovery infinite while protecting the creator's IP through streaming royalties. The artist still owns the music. The listener gets a personalized path to it.

Bookmarked wants to do for books what Spotify did for music: make discovery personalized and infinite while the author and publisher still own the content. But Spotify doesn't have to worry about age-appropriate content laws varying by state, or about being accused of pushing harmful content, or about the personal accountability that comes with recommending the wrong book to the wrong reader. Bookmarked has to solve discovery inside a regulatory landscape that's complex, expanding, and high-stakes — and that's why this problem is worth solving.

Regulatory Complexity

A student in a Texas elementary school has different legal access than a high school senior in Oregon. The matching engine must operate within jurisdiction-specific rules.

Content Sensitivity

Books don't have rating systems, and creating one risks being seen as censorship. The platform must enable informed choice without gatekeeping.

Copyright Boundaries

Until publishers grant access, we can't read the book. We build intelligence from public signals first, then earn publisher trust for deeper access.

Trust From All Sides

Parents must trust we're not pushing content. Librarians must trust we're not replacing them. Publishers must trust we're not pirating. Districts must trust we're legally defensible.

The Parent Connection

Parents have always wanted to know what their kids are reading. Now, increasingly, they have the right to. Across the country, legislation is shifting toward a "parents first" model — giving families more visibility and more voice in what books are available to their children. That's a good thing. The question is: what tools exist to make that real?

OnShelf Engage makes parents informed participants in their child's reading life — not surprised objectors showing up at board meetings:

  • Visibility — see what books are in your child's library and what they've checked out
  • Intelligence — understand what's in a book without having to read it: themes, age appropriateness, content context, educational value
  • Agency — restrict specific titles for their child, without removing the book from the library for everyone else
  • Connection (coming soon) — critical thinking guides so parents can engage with their kids about what they're reading, ask meaningful questions, and encourage more reading — without having to read every book themselves
  • Voice — weigh in on proposed book orders with feedback that's tracked and attached to the specific book

This isn't about control. It's about connection. A parent who can see what their child is reading — and have a conversation about it — is a parent who's engaged in their child's education. OnShelf Engage turns the growing expectation of parent involvement into an opportunity for families to read together, think together, and grow together.

The Publisher Opportunity

The traditional book distribution model is consolidating, with distributors taking 40-55% margins and offering publishers limited data on what's actually happening on library shelves. Publishers need new, intelligent channels to institutional buyers — and Bookmarked is building the first data-driven distribution channel in publishing.

Compliance Channel

Publishers grant Bookmarked access to read their books. The AI assesses regulatory compliance — giving districts definitive answers instead of guesswork. Publishers become part of the solution, not the problem.

Demand Intelligence

Bookmarked sees every book in every library, every collection gap, every student demographic. "3,200 elementary libraries have zero books on climate science for grades 3-5" — that's a signal no publisher has ever had. We become the intelligence that drives informed ordering.

Personalized Previews

Publishers print one message on the back of a book for a million readers. Bookmarked creates a unique, informed preview for every reader — "here's why YOU would love this book" — without compromising the copyrighted text.

Beachhead Strategy

Why School Districts First

School districts are not the ceiling. They are the beachhead — and the people inside them are drowning. Underfunded, understaffed, over-regulated, and using tools built for a world that no longer exists. They didn't sign up to be personally accountable for every book in every classroom. But here they are.

⚖️

Regulatory Forcing Function

26 states and growing have book compliance legislation creating immediate, urgent need. Districts must comply — and the requirements keep expanding. They don't have the tools to manage what's being asked of them.

📋

Book Order Chaos

Imagine ordering 800 books on a spreadsheet. Who gives feedback? Where does it go? Who tracks it? Who authorizes it? What evidence accompanies each decision? Many states now require parent review, committee review, and board approval for every book ordered. Districts are doing this with Excel and Google Forms.

🏫

Classroom Library Crisis

Superintendents are now accountable for books in hundreds of classrooms they've never inventoried. Teachers in many states can no longer put books in their classrooms without approval. No tool exists to give districts visibility into what's actually on classroom shelves. OnShelf is the first.

📊

Data Acquisition Strategy

Every district that joins contributes to the network intelligence layer — challenge outcomes, placement patterns, ordering decisions. This dataset doesn't exist anywhere else. It compounds with every district added.

📚

Publisher Proof Point

When hundreds of districts use Bookmarked for purchasing decisions, publishers have a compelling reason to participate: a direct, intelligent, data-driven channel to institutional buyers.

🤖

Impossible Without AI

A librarian cannot read 10,000 books, know 500 students, track regulatory changes across states, and curate a living collection. This was already impossible. The new regulatory requirements just made the stakes higher and the margin for error smaller.

The structural insight: Everyone else builds individual tools for individual problems — a challenge tracker here, a catalog tool there. The data stays segregated. Districts use Excel spreadsheets, Google Forms, and manual processes stitched together with hope. Bookmarked is the unified platform — all the data, all the books, all the regulatory requirements, all the stakeholders — in one place where intelligence can actually work.

The economic argument: Districts historically solved problems by adding people. Budgets are shrinking. They need to give time back, not add headcount. Bookmarked makes existing staff effective at the right things — letting AI do the work humans literally cannot do, so humans can do the work only humans should do.

The Other Direction

Protecting Books That Should Be There

Most people hear "book compliance" and think about keeping inappropriate content away from kids. That's half the story. The other half — and arguably the more important half — is protecting books from wrongful removal.

Censorship doesn't always look like a government ban. It looks like:

  • A librarian who never orders a book because it's on a ban list — even though the ban was reversed in the original district
  • A district that removes a book because one parent complained and nobody wants the fight — even though the book is legally appropriate for that grade level
  • A publisher whose book dies quietly because it appears on a list nobody fact-checks
  • A child who never discovers a book that would have changed their life, because the adults around them made decisions without data

Bookmarked cuts both directions. We help districts keep inappropriate content out AND keep appropriate content in. Our intelligence isn't just about risk — it's about evidence. When a book is challenged, we track the outcome. When a book is removed, we can show whether that decision was based on law, policy, or opinion. Over time, Bookmarked builds the "case law" for book decisions that doesn't exist anywhere today.

This is what makes us agnostic. We don't take a political position on any book. We provide the intelligence — sourced, traceable, defensible — and let humans make informed decisions. That protects everyone: the book, the reader, the educator, and the institution.
