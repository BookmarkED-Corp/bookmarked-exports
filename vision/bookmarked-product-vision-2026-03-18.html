What OnShelf Is

OnShelf is the unified platform where school districts manage their entire book ecosystem — every library, every classroom, every book order, every compliance requirement, every stakeholder — in one place, powered by Book Intelligence.

Districts currently manage this across disconnected tools: Excel spreadsheets for book orders, Google Forms for parent feedback, legacy library systems (Follett Destiny, Alexandria) for circulation, PEN America ban lists for compliance checks, email chains for committee reviews, and manual processes stitched together with hope. None of these tools talk to each other. The data exists, but it's segregated — so decisions get made without the full picture, and the work takes exponentially longer than it should.

What OnShelf Is Not

  • Not a library catalog system — we don't replace the ILS. We sit on top of it, adding the intelligence and workflow layer that catalogs were never designed to provide.
  • Not a content filter or censor — we provide intelligence for informed human decisions. We never restrict, approve, or remove a book. The human always decides.
  • Not a book distributor — we don't sell or distribute books. We help districts make better, faster, data-informed decisions about what to order and what belongs on their shelves.
  • Not a reading app — students don't read books inside OnShelf. We manage the ecosystem around reading — the collections, the intelligence, the compliance, and the discovery.
Section 2

Book Intelligence: The Engine

Book Intelligence is Bookmarked's proactive scanning and data aggregation system that continuously searches the internet for publicly available information about books — gathering intelligence that would otherwise require a librarian to research every book individually, one at a time, with no guarantee of accuracy.

It is NOT a module or a tab. It is the engine underneath every surface in OnShelf. When a librarian views their collection, Book Intelligence powers the content assessments and flags. When a teacher submits a book for ordering, Book Intelligence surfaces what the district needs to know. When a parent views a book, Book Intelligence provides the transparency.

How It Works Today

Book Intelligence scans the internet continuously, looking for information about books across hundreds of sources — news outlets, advocacy organizations, school district websites, board meeting records, professional review publications, and open metadata databases. When it finds relevant information, it flags the book and presents that information to the district.

Critical distinction: When Book Intelligence shows that a book has been "banned" or "challenged," it is reporting what others have marked — not making that determination itself. There is no single source of truth anywhere in the world for whether a book is definitively banned or challenged. PEN America tracks challenges but not resolutions. A book removed for review and returned to the shelf still appears "banned" on their list. Book Intelligence aggregates more context than any single source, but the district always makes the final determination based on the evidence presented.

What Book Intelligence Provides (Per Book)

Identity & Metadata

Title, author, publisher, ISBN (edition-specific), cover image, page count, publication year, synopsis from authoritative sources (ISBNDB, Open Library), subject classifications (dual-sourced), genre tags, theme tags, people/characters, setting/time/place.

Content Assessment

Content ratings (Violence, Sexual Content, Language — None/Mild/Moderate/Explicit), mature themes identification (e.g., Totalitarianism, Surveillance, Psychological Manipulation), audience & reading level (age range, grade level, reading level), grade level suitability (Elementary/Middle/High School — Suitable/Not Suitable), educational value assessment, content warnings, parental guidance recommendations.

Flags — The Proactive Alert System

The heart of Book Intelligence today. Flags are organized by source type: Library/Advocacy Organizations (ALA, PEN America, state-level organizations), News/Media Outlets (articles, reporting, opinion pieces), and School/District Websites (board minutes, policy documents, district actions). Each flag includes date, category, description, source URL, state, and district when available.

Flags surface automatically and continuously. A librarian doesn't have to go looking — if something is being said about a book in their collection, Bookmarked finds it and brings it to them. This replaces hours of manual research per book with proactive, sourced, traceable intelligence.

Collaborative Network Notes

Librarians and administrators can add notes to any book — context, observations, review outcomes, committee decisions. These notes are shared across the entire Bookmarked network, creating collaborative intelligence that no other system provides. A librarian in one district benefits from the research and decisions of librarians in other districts. This is human-powered network intelligence, live today.

Section 3

The Intelligence Layer Architecture

Book Intelligence operates across three layers, each building on the last. The layers represent increasing depth of understanding — from what people are saying about a book, to what the network reveals about it, to what's actually inside it.

Live Today

Layer 1 — External Signals

Publicly available intelligence gathered from across the internet: challenge/ban reports (PEN America, state-level trackers, district websites), news coverage, board meeting minutes, professional reviews (Kirkus, SLJ, Booklist), advocacy organizations (ALA), legislative tracking, and open metadata databases (ISBNDB, Open Library).

What districts see: The Book Intelligence card on every book — content assessments, flags organized by source, audience guidance, educational value, advisory context. All sourced, all traceable, all continuously updated.

The PEN America problem: PEN America tracks challenges but not resolutions. A book returned to the shelf still shows "banned." Districts refuse to order anything on the list. Result: de facto censorship from incomplete data. Bookmarked captures more context than any single source — and as the network grows, it will track outcomes, creating the case law that doesn't exist anywhere today.

Building

Layer 2 — Network Intelligence

Intelligence generated by the Bookmarked network itself — decisions, patterns, and outcomes across all districts using the platform. Some elements are live today (collaborative notes sharing), others are being built.

  • Collaborative notes (live): Librarians share context about books across the entire network
  • Challenge velocity: Which titles are being reviewed across districts right now?
  • Placement patterns: Where are books being shelved? How do similar districts handle the same title?
  • Decision benchmarking: What did districts like yours decide about this book?
  • Review outcomes: Approved, restricted, relocated, removed — outcome data that creates the "case law" for book decisions
  • Ordering patterns: What are similar districts ordering? What's working for their student populations?

Privacy architecture: Individual district data stays private. The network sees aggregated patterns and anonymized benchmarks. Districts get collective intelligence without exposing their specific decisions.

Why this is defensible: The network effect creates a moat that deepens with every district added. A new entrant would need to rebuild this dataset from scratch — years of decisions across hundreds of districts.

Future

Layer 3 — Content Scanner (Publisher Content)

Source-verified intelligence from reading actual book content. The Content Scanner — AI-powered analysis of what's actually inside the book — requires publisher copyright access.

  • Definitive content assessment — not "this book was reported as challenged" but "here is exactly what content appears and where"
  • Regulatory compliance verification against specific state and district-level requirements
  • Personalized book previews for every reader — without revealing copyrighted text
  • Student-book matching based on actual content, reading interests, and regulatory context
  • Collection gap analysis based on content diversity, not just metadata
  • Protection both directions — definitive evidence for whether a book should or shouldn't be in a specific setting

Why publishers will participate: The traditional book distribution model is under pressure — consolidation, high margins (40-55%), and limited data flowing back to publishers. Publishers need new, intelligent channels to institutional buyers. Bookmarked offers three things no one else can: a compliance channel (regulatory confidence), demand intelligence (collection gap data across thousands of libraries), and personalized previews (one message per reader instead of one message per million).

Section 4

The Time Equation

Bookmarked is not a compliance platform. Compliance is built into the platform. The real value is the time we give back.

Most districts believe they're already in compliance — and technically, many can check that box. The problem is their compliance isn't backed by data-informed decisions. They have lists of books they're required to maintain, but no data to help them make informed decisions about those books. The result: an extraordinary amount of time spent on manual research, committee coordination, feedback management, and documentation — time that should be spent on discovery, curation, and connecting students with books they'll love.

40 hrs/mo
Time large districts spend managing book orders, reviews, and board preparation
~$375/day
Average cost of a librarian's time

That's a librarian spending an entire work week every month just managing the ordering and approval process — on spreadsheets, with no system of record, across disconnected tools. Even districts spending half that time are losing days every month to work that should take hours.

OnShelf doesn't add work. Every feature is designed to reduce the time to make good decisions. Book Intelligence replaces hours of manual research per book. OnShelf Review replaces spreadsheet chaos with a single workflow. Classroom Libraries replace invisible risk with instant visibility. The goal: a librarian does in 45 minutes what used to take days.

This is how we save districts money — not by adding headcount to the problem, but by making existing staff effective at the right things. The time given back to compliance can be redirected to what librarians actually want to do: connect students with books.

OnShelf Review (Orders)
Live

The complete book order and review workflow — replacing the patchwork of Excel spreadsheets, email chains, Google Forms, and manual processes that districts have been forced to cobble together. Before OnShelf, ordering 800 books meant managing spreadsheets across dozens of teachers, researching every title individually, tracking committee feedback in email threads nobody could find, collecting parent input through Google Forms with no way to attach it to specific books, and presenting the board with a printout that provided no evidence or audit trail.

OnShelf Review brings it all under one roof:

  • Three-stage pipeline: "Books to Order" → "Books to Review for Order" → "Book Orders" — clear stages with clear ownership
  • Intelligence inline: Every book in an order list shows its Book Intelligence — summary, age range, ban count, challenge count, theme tags. Ban and challenge details expandable per book (which district, which school, why, source link, date)
  • Notes per book: Reviewers add context, concerns, and recommendations directly on the book in the order list
  • Committee workflow: Route to SLAC or library committee for review when the district requires it
  • Parent review: Push a curated list to a public URL for the required parent comment period. Parent feedback is tracked and attached to the specific book — not lost in a Google Form
  • Board approval: Route finalized orders to the board with full documentation and evidence at every stage
  • Complete audit trail: Every action documented. Every decision defensible. If a book is questioned next year, the district can show exactly who reviewed it, what evidence was considered, and how the decision was made
Teacher

Submit book order requests with the confidence that intelligence will be applied automatically.

Librarian

Curate orders, review against Book Intelligence, collaborate with administrators, prepare for committee review.

Committee / Parents / Board

Review with evidence, provide tracked feedback, approve with documentation.

What "great" looks like: A teacher submits 15 books. The librarian reviews them with intelligence inline — 12 clean, 2 with challenge history worth noting, 1 with content concerns. She adds notes, collaborates with her administrator, and submits for committee review. The entire process — which used to take days of research and coordination — takes 30 minutes of informed decision-making.

4
Classroom Libraries
Live

Before OnShelf, classroom libraries were a black hole. Superintendents didn't know what books were in hundreds of classrooms across their district. Teachers had the freedom to put whatever they wanted on their shelves. Now, in many states, that freedom has been replaced by approval requirements — and the superintendent is personally accountable for content they've never seen, in rooms they've never inventoried.

OnShelf makes the invisible visible:

  • Unified visibility: Administrators see every classroom library under every school — all the books, all the flags, all in one place
  • Grade-level controls: Books are designated for specific grade levels. If a teacher in an elementary school tries to add a book designated for middle school, the system requires librarian or administrator approval before it's allowed
  • Cross-referencing: See if a book already exists in the school library before ordering a duplicate for a classroom — or identify a flagged book that's in a classroom but not in the main library's review queue
  • Teacher experience: Each teacher manages their own classroom library using the same interface as the main library — familiar, simple, with the same Book Intelligence applied to every title
  • District-wide policy: Administrators can establish consistent policies and workflows across all classrooms, removing ambiguity about what's allowed and what requires review
  • Book Intelligence applied everywhere: The same flags, content ratings, and intelligence that protect the school library also protect every classroom

What "great" looks like: A superintendent opens the dashboard and sees that across 266 classroom libraries, 201 books are flagged. She drills into one elementary classroom and finds a book designated for high school. She flags it for the librarian, who routes it for review — all documented, all defensible. Before OnShelf, she wouldn't have known the book existed until a parent complained.

5
OnShelf Engage (Parent/Guardian Access)
Live

Across the country, legislation is shifting toward a "parents first" model — giving families more visibility and more voice in what books are available to their children. OnShelf Engage makes parents informed participants in their child's reading life — not surprised objectors showing up at board meetings.

  • Visibility: See what books are available in their child's school library AND classroom libraries — not just the main collection, but every shelf their child has access to
  • Checkouts: See what books their child has checked out, with activity tracking
  • Intelligence: Understand what's in a book without having to read it — themes, content context, age appropriateness, educational value
  • Restrictions: Restrict specific titles for their child without removing the book from the library for everyone else. The restriction is documented and the librarian is notified
  • Order review: During the parent comment period, review proposed book orders with summaries, intelligence, and the ability to provide feedback that's tracked and attached to the specific book

What this replaces: Showing up at a board meeting angry because they didn't know what was in the library. Google Forms with no follow-up. Feeling unheard. OnShelf Engage turns a regulatory requirement into an opportunity for families to be connected to their child's reading life.

Reports
Live

Comprehensive reporting engine organized into five categories:

  • Circulation Reports: Book circulation tracking, collection growth trends, flagged books reporting
  • Collection Reports: Complete inventory, never-checked-out books, genre distribution, collection age analysis, new acquisitions, books with parent/guardian restrictions
  • Student Activity: Top readers, students with no checkouts, restriction summary

Backend student data management — not a student-facing interface. Students do not log in or interact with OnShelf directly. This module controls access and visibility:

  • Access control: Ensures librarians see students in their school, teachers see students in their classrooms. No one sees students they shouldn't have access to.
  • Student profiles: View individual student data — what books they've checked out, what restrictions their parents have set, checkout history
  • Restriction visibility: When a parent restricts a book through OnShelf Engage, the restriction is visible here so librarians and teachers can respect it during checkout
8
Circulation Management
Live

Book checkout, check-in, approvals, and circulation history. The interface for librarians to manage the physical flow of books — request checkout (by student and book), process returns, manage approval queues, and view circulation history. This is the operational layer that generates the usage data visible in the dashboard and reports.

The Proven Process — Discover > Protect > Defend > Optimize > Connect

These five actions represent how districts experience the Bookmarked platform — though not necessarily in linear order. A district might enter through Protect (compliance), gain Defend (audit trail and documentation), discover they need to Discover (see what's actually in their libraries), then move toward Optimize (better ordering, better collections) and eventually Connect (matching students to books). The product supports all five — the district chooses where to start based on their most urgent need.

Section 7

Product Principles

These guide what we build, what we don't build, and how we make trade-offs.

1

Give Time Back, Don't Add Work

Every feature must reduce the time to make good decisions. If a feature adds work, it fails. Librarians are spending 40 hours a month on ordering processes alone. Our job is to give that time back — not add another tool to learn.

2

Intelligence at the Point of Decision

Book Intelligence appears where decisions happen — not as a separate research step. Inline in the order review. Inline in the library view. Never make someone leave their workflow to access intelligence.

3

Data-Backed Decisions Replace Bias

Librarians choosing 10,000 from 40+ million is a data problem. Publishers recommending their own books is commercial bias. PEN America marking "banned" without tracking resolutions is incomplete data. We don't provide bias. We provide data. Every decision on our platform can be informed by evidence, not opinion.

4

Informed Choice, Not Gatekeeping

We provide intelligence that enables informed human decisions. We never restrict, approve, or remove a book. We equip people with data and let them decide. Bookmarked is not a censor.

5

Agnostic Platform

No commercial bias (we don't sell books). No personal bias (we don't curate by opinion). No political bias (both "restrict" and "protect" legislation creates demand). Intelligence is sourced, traceable, and objective.

6

Protect Both Directions

Protect against books that shouldn't be there (regulatory compliance). Also protect books that SHOULD be there — identifying removals driven by opinion or bias rather than legitimate grounds. Evidence, not opinion, in both directions.

7

Defensibility by Design

Every action creates an audit trail. Every decision is documented. This isn't a feature — it's the architectural principle. When a book is questioned next year, the district can show exactly what happened.

8

Unified, Not Fragmented

All data in one place. School library, classroom libraries, orders, intelligence, compliance, parent feedback, board approvals — all connected. The value is the connections between data, not just the data itself.

9

Build the Network, Not Just the Tool

Every district contributes to intelligence that makes the system smarter for everyone. Collaborative notes today. Aggregated patterns tomorrow. Design features that create network data alongside features that consume it.

10

Copyright is Sacred

We never compromise, expose, or diminish the publisher's IP. We build intelligence about and around books. When we have publisher access, we create derivative intelligence, not derivative content.

Section 8

The Compliance-to-Discovery Arc

Compliance opened the door. Discovery is what's on the other side. Each stage builds on the last — and gives time back at every step.

Stage 1
Compliance
"We need to comply — and we don't have the tools."

Where most districts enter. Upload your collection, see your flags, manage orders through the required workflow, document everything. Value: You're in compliance with documentation to prove it. And you just saved your librarian 20+ hours this month.

Stage 2
Visibility
"We had no idea what was in our classroom libraries."

Emerging. Classroom library visibility, grade-level controls, cross-referencing, collection health metrics, dead inventory identification. Value: For the first time, the superintendent sees the entire district's book ecosystem — and can set consistent policies across it.

Stage 3
Optimization
"Based on our students and gaps — what should we order next?"

Next. Collection gap analysis, student-informed recommendations, budget optimization, peer benchmarking, flag synthesis (TLDR across all intelligence). Value: Districts build better libraries with the budget they have — and spend their time curating, not researching.

Stage 4
Network
"What are districts like us doing? What's working?"

Building. Network benchmarks, predictive challenge alerts, peer decisions, the "case law" repository for book decisions. Value: Collective intelligence. Decisions informed by the wisdom of the entire network.

Stage 5
Discovery
"Every student matched to books they'll love."

Future. Student-book matching, personalized reading paths, Content Scanner intelligence, collection curation based on actual students. Value: Libraries thrive. Literacy improves. Compliance is automatic. The time we gave back is now spent on what matters — connecting every reader with their next great book.

Appendix: Intelligence Card Anatomy

What users see on every book detail page (based on live product — 1984 by George Orwell):

Header

Book cover, title, author, location tag (e.g., "School Library"), ISBN, publisher, year, pages. Action buttons: Set Status, Refresh Metadata, Delete Book. Flag count badge (e.g., "36 Flags").

Left Navigation

Book Intelligence (main view) · Guardian Activities · Status History · Circulation History · Notes and Documents

Intelligence Sections

  1. Synopsis — From ISBNDB, including notable recognitions (e.g., "PBS's The Great American Read")
  2. Subjects — Dual-sourced from ISBNDB and Open Library for comprehensive coverage
  3. People/Characters — Named characters and referenced people in the work
  4. Times/Setting/Place — When available
  5. Flags — Tabbed by source type (Library/Advocacy Organizations, News/Media Outlets, School/District Websites). Each flag: date, category, description, source link, state, district. Represents what others have reported — not Bookmarked's determination.
  6. Audience & Reading Level — Age range, grade level, reading level
  7. Content Ratings — Violence, Sexual Content, Language (None/Mild/Moderate/Explicit) + mature themes tags
  8. Compliance & Challenge Risk — Reported ban status, challenge risk level, potential concerns
  9. Grade Level Suitability — Elementary/Middle/High School (Suitable/Not Suitable)
  10. Advisory & Educational Context — Parental guidance recommendation, content warnings, themes, educational value narrative
