Social Media Audit: Book Intelligence Messaging

March 20, 2026 — Research audit of how Book Intelligence is currently discussed across Bookmarked's social and web presence

Executive Summary

Book Intelligence is Bookmarked's core strategic differentiator, but social media is barely telling the story.

Across 9 connected social accounts (Postiz), a company blog, Steve's personal blog, and the company website, the Book Intelligence narrative is fragmented, compliance-heavy, and missing the most powerful elements of the vision: the three-layer architecture, network intelligence effects, ground truth advantage, and the founder's personal story.

The gap between what the internal vision documents describe and what the world sees is enormous. The internal product vision is compelling, architecturally rich, and emotionally resonant. The external social presence is sparse, compliance-focused, and tells none of the intelligence story.

VII. Narrative Assessment

Current Narrative Being Told

The story the world hears: Bookmarked is a Texas compliance tool that helps school districts manage book challenges under SB 13. It saves librarians time on paperwork.

The dominant frame: Compliance automation. Operational efficiency. Texas-specific legislative response.

The risk: This positions Bookmarked as a point solution tied to a single law in a single state. When press coverage frames the company, it defaults to the political narrative ("book ban technology") because Bookmarked hasn't provided a more compelling alternative narrative.

Narrative That Should Be Told

The story that exists in the vision documents: Bookmarked is building the intelligence layer between every book and every reader. Not a compliance tool — an entirely new category: Book Intelligence. A three-layer architecture that starts with external signals, builds network intelligence from 150+ districts, and will eventually read books with publisher permission. The company that creates the "case law" for book challenges that doesn't exist anywhere today. Founded by a high school dropout with dyslexia whose daughter showed him what personalized book discovery looks like.

This is a category-defining story. None of it is being told on social media.

VIII. Specific Messaging Elements — Detailed Status

Three-Layer Architecture Not Communicated

The three-layer model (External Signals → Network Intelligence → Content Scanner) is the most compelling technical narrative in the company's arsenal. It explains the roadmap, the moat, the data flywheel, and the publisher strategy in one framework. It has never appeared on any public social channel, blog post, or web page.

Network Intelligence Story Not Communicated

"Intelligence improves with every district" — the network effect story — is the moat narrative investors, press, and sophisticated buyers need to hear. Concepts like challenge velocity, decision benchmarking, and placement patterns are entirely internal. The "73% of similar districts retained this title" example would be one of the most powerful social media posts Bookmarked could publish. It doesn't exist.

Ground Truth Advantage Not Communicated

The PEN America problem — "they track bans but not resolutions, creating de facto blacklists" — is a genuinely novel insight that positions Bookmarked as the source of truth. The "case law for book challenges" framing is category-defining language. None of this has been published externally.

ChatGPT Comparison Partially Communicated

The November 2025 blog post ("Why Using ChatGPT to Review Books Is Risky") addresses this but leads with fear (lawyers fined $5,000, hallucination risk) rather than positive differentiation. The internal product vision has a brilliant 8-dimension comparison table (consistency, source attribution, ISBN fidelity, defensibility, collaboration, institutional memory, continuous monitoring, workflow integration) that would make an excellent LinkedIn carousel or infographic. It's never been adapted for social.

Founder Story Partially Communicated

The founder narrative is the most emotionally resonant content Bookmarked has. The Bic pen moment, the closet, the report card documenting declining reading interest, Faith as the first "Book Intelligence engine" — this is the kind of content that goes viral on LinkedIn. It was captured internally in March 2026 but has never been deployed as social content. A brief dyslexia mention on LinkedIn is the only trace.

IX. Competitive Positioning in Social

Search visibility for "Book Intelligence": When someone searches "Book Intelligence" + school library, bookmarked.com ranks. But the search results show the compliance page, not an intelligence-as-a-category explanation.

Narrative control: The Fast Company article ("The startup that turned Texas's book ban law into big business") is currently the dominant external narrative. Without proactive social media thought leadership, the press fills the narrative vacuum with the most clickable angle — which is political controversy, not intelligence innovation.

Perception risk: If the only story being told is compliance + Texas + book bans, Bookmarked gets boxed into a niche. The intelligence platform story — the Spotify analogy, the three layers, the network effects — is the only thing that breaks out of that box.

XI. Top 5 Gaps (Priority Order)

1. Steve's personal LinkedIn is dormant. This is the single highest-impact, lowest-cost fix. Founder-led content on LinkedIn gets 5-10x the reach of company pages. The founder narrative is already written — it just needs to become social posts.

2. The three-layer architecture has never been explained publicly. This is the category-defining framework. A single LinkedIn carousel or blog post explaining "External Signals → Network Intelligence → Content Scanner" would reposition every conversation about the company.

3. Network intelligence is Bookmarked's moat and nobody knows it. "What did districts like yours decide about this book?" is a powerful concept that's entirely locked inside internal documents. This should be a recurring social theme.

4. Zero video content. YouTube is empty. No explainer videos, no founder story videos, no demo walkthroughs. A 2-minute "What is Book Intelligence?" video would be worth more than months of text posts.

5. Compliance messaging dominates over intelligence/vision messaging. The messaging hierarchy says library vitality first, compliance as supporting evidence. Social content inverts this — most posts lead with compliance. The "intelligence platform" positioning isn't landing because it's not being pushed.
