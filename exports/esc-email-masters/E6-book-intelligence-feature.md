Dear Dr. [Recipient Name],

I want to share the thing about Bookmarked that really set them apart for us when we evaluated this space.

Most tools help districts catalog and manage books. Bookmarked helps districts actually understand them. That difference matters more than it sounds.

At the core of their platform is something called Book Intelligence — an engine that continuously gathers what public sources say about every book in your collection. For each title, your team sees what external organizations have flagged, context from public reviews, metadata, and community discussion about the book, and what peer districts across the Bookmarked network have decided about the same book. All of it organized for the decision at hand, not buried in a research project.

Here's what that changes in practice: when a teacher submits a book order, every title gets checked automatically. Your librarian sees flags and context before making a recommendation — not after a parent raises a concern. When a book goes to committee review, the intelligence is already assembled. When the board asks questions, there's a documented record.

I want to be clear about what this isn't. Bookmarked doesn't tell your district which books are appropriate. That professional judgment belongs to your librarians and your community. What Book Intelligence does is make sure those decisions are informed by the best available data instead of improvised Google searches. Legacy systems list books. Bookmarked understands books.
