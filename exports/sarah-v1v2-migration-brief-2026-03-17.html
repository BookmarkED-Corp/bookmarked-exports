When this is complete, the following should all be true:

  • ✓ A single Confluence page (Product Hub > V1 to V2 Migration) contains the complete plan
  • ✓ All existing notes, ideas, and context are merged into that page — nothing left scattered

HOW TO APPROACH THIS

Use the Agents. That's the Whole Point.

The CPO, scrum master, and product intelligence agents have frameworks and expertise baked in. Your job isn't to write this plan from scratch — it's to direct the agents to build it, then QA and refine their output until it's right.

Think of it like managing a team of specialists. You bring the context and product knowledge. They bring the structure and methodology. Together you produce something better than either could alone.

1

Gather everything first

Before you open a Claude thread, collect all the inputs: the V1 Data Transfer Discovery page (Patrick's API crawl — 70 endpoints, 14 risks), any notes you and Patrick have about migration, Slack conversations, Jira tickets related to V1/V2. Have it all ready.

2

Load the right skill expert

Open Claude Code. Before asking it to do anything, bring in the domain expert. Type /cpo to load the CPO agent. Tell it: "I need to build a V1 to V2 migration architecture. Here's what I have. What's the right framework for this?" Let the agent propose the approach.

3

Feed it your context

Point the agent to the V1 Data Transfer Discovery page in Confluence (page 88834054). Share Patrick's findings. Share your own knowledge about which districts are complex, which are straightforward, what the common gotchas are. The agent needs your product knowledge to produce something real.

4

QA the output — really QA it

Don't accept the first draft. Spend 30-45 minutes reading through it carefully. Ask yourself: if someone handed me this plan and I'd never seen it before, would I know exactly what to do? Where are the gaps? What assumptions did it make? Push back. Ask follow-up questions. Refine.

5

Write it to Confluence

The plan goes into the V1 to V2 Migration page in the Product Hub. Not in a Google Doc, not in Slack, not in your head. Confluence is where the team and the agents can find it, reference it, and build on it.

6

Request a QA pass

Once you're satisfied with the plan, ask Claude (or ask Steve to have Claude) run a quality check against it. Look for missing steps, unaddressed risks, districts that might be edge cases. A good plan gets better when someone tries to break it.

When to Ask for a New Agent

If you're working on the migration plan and you hit a point where none of the existing agents have the right expertise — maybe you need a data migration specialist that understands schema mapping, or a district onboarding expert that knows the enrollment workflow inside out — don't settle for generic Claude doing a mediocre job.

Flag it. Tell Steve: "I need an agent that knows X because the existing agents don't cover this well enough." We'll build it. A purpose-built agent with the right knowledge will produce dramatically better output than a general-purpose model guessing its way through.

Identifying the gaps in our agent ecosystem is one of the most valuable things you can do in this process.

YOUR PLAN, YOUR OWNERSHIP

A Note on How This Works

This brief gives you the outcome and the tools. The approach is yours to own.

If you sit down with the agents and the plan that emerges looks different from what's outlined here — that might be better. You have product context and customer knowledge that isn't captured anywhere. If the agents propose a migration sequence and you know from experience that certain districts are going to be problematic, that's exactly the kind of input that makes a plan real instead of theoretical.

What matters is:

  1. The plan is complete and well-thought-out. Someone else should be able to read it and understand the full picture — the what, the why, the sequence, the risks, and the open questions.
  2. It lives in Confluence. Not in a conversation, not in your notes. In the shared place where the team and the agents can reference it.
  3. You used the agents to build it. Not because we're mandating a workflow, but because the agents have frameworks and pattern-matching that will make the output stronger. Your job is to direct them well and QA their work rigorously.
  4. If your plan differs from expectations, you can explain why. That's a sign of good thinking, not a problem. Share it with Steve for review and walk through your reasoning.

The One Thing to Avoid

Don't default to doing this manually — writing it stream-of-consciousness, scattering it across tools, moving on before it's fully scoped. Take the time. Sit with it. An hour of deep, focused work with the agents will produce a plan that saves weeks of confusion downstream. The V1 Data Transfer Discovery page is proof — that level of rigor is the bar.
