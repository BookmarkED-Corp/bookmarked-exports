Blog Post 1 — District Spotlight

How Connally ISD Took a Book List to the Board — and Got Zero Complaints

Website blog draft | ~650 words | Voice: Trusted Guide | Pillar: Proof & Traction + Library Vitality

Before their last board meeting, Dr. Sandra Hancock ran Connally ISD's proposed book list through Bookmarked, confirmed nothing was flagged, and presented it to the board with confidence. The board posted the list publicly for parent feedback. The result: zero complaints during the entire public comment period.

Across Texas, that outcome is rarer than it should be. Districts are navigating a compliance landscape where a single parent challenge can escalate into a board meeting crisis. Some have stopped ordering new library books entirely — choosing silence over the risk of a misstep.

Connally ISD took a different path.

Dr. Hancock — the district's Assistant Superintendent of Curriculum and Instruction — describes the process as simple: run the proposed titles through Bookmarked, review anything that gets flagged, and move forward with clarity.

"It was much faster than if I would have to do that on my own, number by number." — Dr. Sandra Hancock, Assistant Superintendent of Curriculum & Instruction, Connally ISD

For Dr. Hancock, the speed matters — but the confidence matters more. Without the right tools, the weight of the current political climate makes every book decision feel high-stakes.

"It is a scary proposition when you realize what the political climate is. If I didn't have this product, like — this has really simplified and taken my stress level much lower." — Dr. Sandra Hancock

When the book list went to the board, Dr. Hancock was able to tell them it had been vetted. The board posted it publicly for community feedback — and not a single parent complaint came through.

"I think our folks felt much more at ease knowing that it's been vetted." — Dr. Sandra Hancock

But Dr. Hancock knows Connally's experience isn't the norm. She watches neighboring districts navigate the same compliance pressures — some facing waves of parent challenges, others frozen in place, unwilling to order new titles at all.

"That just breaks my heart — to think of just not ordering books. We're here as public educators because we want to help kids, and we want to have a well-read, well-educated citizenry that can make decisions." — Dr. Sandra Hancock

When asked what she would say to a superintendent at a district currently dealing with parent challenges, Dr. Hancock didn't hesitate:

"It meets both sides of the fence. It meets the parents who want their children to have access, and it meets the ones who want to limit or protect. It feels like that win-win solution that gives parents the information and just puts it back in their hands." — Dr. Sandra Hancock

Connally ISD's approach is a model for what's possible when districts have the right tools. Instead of choosing between access and compliance, they chose both. The board has confidence. Parents have transparency. And the books made it onto the shelves — which is the whole point.

Blog Post 2 — Thought Leadership

The Books That Never Get Ordered

Website blog draft | By Steve Wandler, Founder | Voice: Visionary Authority → Trusted Guide | Pillar: Library Vitality + Industry & Policy

Something quiet is happening in school districts across Texas. No headlines. No board meeting blowups. Just — silence. Districts are choosing not to order new library books.

Not because the books are bad. Not because the budgets dried up. Because the compliance burden has become so heavy that the safest move feels like no move at all.

This is the real cost of the current legislative landscape. Not the high-profile parent challenges that make the news. The invisible ones — the book orders that never happen, the collections that go stale, the students who walk into a library and find nothing new on the shelves.

Fear Shouldn't Be the Default

When we talk to district leaders, the pattern is consistent. The legislation created real obligations — SB 13 requires review processes, SB 412 introduced personal liability for school personnel. These are serious requirements that deserve serious responses. But the response too many districts have landed on is paralysis.

Staff who aren't certified librarians are placed in library roles and told to figure it out. They're scared. They don't want the headache. So they stop ordering. The shelves stay where they are. The students lose.

Dr. Sandra Hancock, Assistant Superintendent of Curriculum and Instruction at Connally ISD, put it plainly:

"It just devastates me that this has become a barrier. People are just fearful of making the misstep." — Dr. Sandra Hancock, Connally ISD

What Solving This Actually Looks Like

Connally ISD didn't stop ordering books. Before their last board meeting, Dr. Hancock ran the proposed titles through Bookmarked, took the list to the board with confidence, and posted it publicly for parent feedback. Zero complaints came through. Not one.

"It meets both sides of the fence. It meets the parents who want their children to have access, and it meets the ones who want to limit or protect. It feels like that win-win solution that gives parents the information and just puts it back in their hands." — Dr. Sandra Hancock, Connally ISD

The Win-Win That's Been Missing

The compliance conversation has been framed as a zero-sum game — either books stay on shelves or parents get control. That framing is wrong, and it's part of why districts are stuck.

When a district knows what's publicly known about every book in their collection — flags, challenge history, peer district decisions — they can make informed, defensible choices. Parents get the information they need. Boards get confidence that the process is sound. And the people managing library collections get their time back for what actually matters.

That's not a compromise. It's what the system should have looked like from the beginning.

Libraries should be the most vibrant rooms in a school. The compliance infrastructure should support that mission, not suffocate it. Districts that have the right tools are proving it's possible to do both — protect and provide.

The districts still frozen in place? They don't have to stay there.
