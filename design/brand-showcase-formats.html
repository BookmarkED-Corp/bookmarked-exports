How to Give Your Librarians Their Time Back

March 17, 2026 · 5 min read
Paige Turner
March 17, 2026

Here's a scenario most superintendents know too well: A parent challenges a book at one of your campuses. What should be a straightforward review turns into weeks of committee meetings, email chains, and manual documentation — all while your librarian's real work sits waiting.

Across Texas, librarians spend 6–8 weeks per challenged title just managing the review process. That's not a compliance problem — it's a time problem. And it's keeping your best people from doing what they were hired to do: building collections that help every student find their next great book.

The Real Cost Isn't Compliance — It's Opportunity

When a librarian spends three hours pulling documentation for a board report, that's three hours they didn't spend on a reading program. When they spend a week coordinating a review committee via email, that's a week of collection development that didn't happen.

The districts we work with don't frame this as a compliance issue. They frame it as a capacity issue. The question isn't "Are we compliant?" — it's "Are we giving our library teams the tools to do their jobs well?"

"Our librarians went from spending half their week on documentation to focusing almost entirely on students. That's the shift that matters."

— Library Director, Temple ISD

What Changes When the Process Is Automated

When book reviews take 2 days instead of 6–8 weeks, the entire dynamic shifts. Librarians get their schedules back. Administrators get board-ready reports without asking for them. And the library program — the thing parents and communities actually care about — gets the attention it deserves.

That's what 150+ Texas districts have already figured out. Not that they needed a compliance tool — but that they needed to stop losing their best people's time to a process that should be automatic.

Start With One Question

Ask your library team how many hours they spend each month on book review documentation. The answer usually surprises people. And it's the clearest signal that there's a better way.
